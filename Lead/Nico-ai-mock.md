
# Mock Interview: Tech Lead

**Candidate:** Nico, a software engineer with 9 years of experience, with the last 3 as a Tech Lead at a travel technology company.

**Interviewer:** Ava, a Senior Director of Engineering at a consumer fintech company.

---

### Introduction

**Ava:** Hi Nico, welcome. I'm Ava, Senior Director of Engineering. I run our consumer products group — mobile apps, web, and the backend services that power them. Could you tell me about your background?

**Nico:** Hi Ava, thanks for having me. I've been building software for about nine years. I started in backend engineering at a media streaming company, working on content delivery and video transcoding pipelines. I moved into a senior role at a travel tech company four years ago and became the Tech Lead for the booking engine team three years ago. I lead a team of seven engineers responsible for our core booking flow — flight search, pricing, seat selection, and payment processing. We handle about 120,000 bookings per day across 400+ airlines. I'm looking for a Tech Lead role at a company where the product directly impacts people's daily financial lives, which is what draws me to fintech.

---

### Behavioral Questions

**Ava:** Great. Let's start with leadership. Describe your leadership style with a specific example.

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** When I joined as Tech Lead, the team had a pattern of building features in isolation — each engineer would take a feature from design through deployment without much collaboration. The code worked, but we had inconsistent patterns across the codebase, knowledge silos where only one person understood each feature, and a bus factor of one on every critical path.
*   **Task:** I wanted to shift the team toward collaborative ownership where knowledge was shared and code patterns were consistent, without slowing down delivery or making engineers feel micromanaged.
*   **Action:** I introduced three practices. First, I started "design pairing" — before any feature that touched more than one service, the owning engineer would spend 30 minutes whiteboarding the approach with one teammate. This wasn't a formal review, just a conversation. Second, I established rotating code ownership areas — every quarter, engineers would swap the subsystem they were primary on-call for, forcing knowledge transfer. Third, I made a habit of asking "who else has context on this?" in every standup. If the answer was "just me," we'd schedule a brief knowledge transfer session. I was careful to frame all of these as team resilience practices, not as oversight.
*   **Result:** Within six months, our bus factor improved from 1 to at least 2 on every critical system. When one engineer took a three-week vacation, the team handled two production issues in their area without needing to contact them — something that would have been impossible before. Code review quality improved because reviewers actually understood the systems they were reviewing. One engineer told me the design pairing sessions were the most useful thing we'd introduced because they caught integration issues before a single line of code was written.

**Ava:** Tell me about a time you had to make a trade-off between people, time, and scope.

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** We were building a new dynamic pricing engine to replace our static fare cache — a major initiative that would let us show real-time prices instead of prices that were up to 4 hours stale. The project was scoped for four months with seven engineers. Two months in, our VP of Product informed us that a major airline partner was threatening to pull their inventory from our platform unless we implemented their new NDC (New Distribution Capability) API within eight weeks. This was non-negotiable — losing this airline would mean losing 30% of our bookings.
*   **Task:** I needed to deliver the airline integration within eight weeks while keeping the pricing engine project alive, without burning out a team that was already fully committed.
*   **Action:** I gathered the team and laid out the situation transparently, including the revenue impact. I then facilitated a planning session where we assessed both projects together. We identified that three of the seven engineers could move to the airline integration full-time — they had the most relevant experience with airline APIs. The remaining four would continue the pricing engine but with a reduced scope: we'd deliver real-time pricing for the top 20 routes (covering 65% of bookings) instead of all routes, deferring the long tail. I also made a commitment to the team that I would personally handle any ad-hoc interruptions during this period so they could stay focused. I worked with the product manager to communicate the revised pricing engine timeline to stakeholders with full transparency about why.
*   **Result:** The airline NDC integration shipped in seven weeks, and the partner renewed their contract. The top-20-routes pricing engine delivered on the original timeline and drove a 12% increase in booking conversion on those routes. The remaining routes were completed over the following quarter. No one worked excessive overtime, and the team's engagement survey scores actually went up because they felt included in the decision-making process.

**Ava:** How do you handle technical disagreements within your team?

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** My team was divided on how to handle session management for our new mobile booking flow. Two engineers wanted to use server-side sessions stored in Redis, arguing it gave us full control over session invalidation — critical for payment flows where you need to prevent double-booking. Two others advocated for stateless JWTs, arguing they'd simplify our horizontal scaling and reduce a Redis dependency that had caused outages before. Both sides had valid security and reliability concerns.
*   **Task:** I needed to help the team converge on a solution that addressed both the payment security requirements and the operational reliability concerns.
*   **Action:** I started by extracting the non-negotiable requirements from the business context: we needed the ability to immediately invalidate sessions (for fraud prevention), we needed to support horizontal scaling without sticky sessions, and we needed to survive a Redis outage without locking all users out of active bookings. I wrote these three requirements on a whiteboard and asked each side to evaluate their preferred approach against all three. This reframed the debate from "Redis vs JWT" to "what architecture satisfies all three constraints?" The team quickly realized that neither pure approach met all requirements. I facilitated a collaborative design session where we arrived at a hybrid: short-lived JWTs (15-minute expiry) for authentication with a lightweight Redis-backed revocation list for immediate invalidation. The revocation list was small enough to cache locally with a 30-second TTL, meaning a Redis outage would only delay revocation by 30 seconds rather than lock users out.
*   **Result:** The hybrid approach satisfied all three requirements and both camps felt their concerns were addressed. The session system has been running for 18 months with zero security incidents and survived two Redis maintenance windows without user impact. More importantly, the process taught the team to start with constraints rather than solutions — they now naturally frame technical debates around "what are the non-negotiables?" before proposing approaches.

**Ava:** Tell me about a time you improved an agile process that wasn't working.

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** Our team's deployments were a source of anxiety. We deployed once a week on Thursdays, batching up a week's worth of changes into a single release. Every deployment felt high-risk — there were 15-20 PRs bundled together, making it hard to isolate issues. When something broke in production, we'd have to sift through a week of changes. Engineers would rush to merge PRs on Wednesday night to "make the train," leading to hasty code reviews. The team dreaded Thursdays.
*   **Task:** I wanted to move the team to a continuous deployment model where deploying was boring and routine, not a weekly event that required bravery.
*   **Action:** I broke the transition into three phases. First, I invested two sprints in improving our CI pipeline and automated test coverage, getting our test suite from 40% to 75% code coverage on critical paths and reducing the CI run time from 25 minutes to 8 minutes. Second, I introduced feature flags using LaunchDarkly so we could deploy code to production without exposing incomplete features to users. Third, I gradually increased deployment frequency — from weekly to twice weekly, then daily, then on-merge. Each phase lasted about three weeks so the team could build confidence. I also set up automated rollback triggers based on error rate spikes in our monitoring. For the first month of on-merge deploys, I was the "deploy buddy" — I'd watch the monitoring dashboards for 15 minutes after each deploy and handle any issues.
*   **Result:** We went from one anxiety-inducing weekly deploy to an average of four deploys per day. Mean time to recovery dropped from 2 hours (finding the bad change in a 20-PR batch) to 8 minutes (rolling back a single PR). Deployment-related incidents decreased by 80%. Engineers started treating deployment as a non-event — one engineer told me "I don't even think about deploys anymore, it just happens." The product team loved it because features reached users within hours of completion instead of waiting for the next Thursday.

**Ava:** Give an example of when you set the direction for a team or project.

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** Our booking engine's error handling was entirely reactive. When an airline API returned an error — a fare expired, a seat was no longer available, a payment was declined by the airline — we'd show the user a generic "something went wrong, please try again" message. Our support team was fielding 300+ calls per day from confused travelers, and our booking completion rate was 15% below industry benchmarks.
*   **Task:** I wanted to transform our error handling from a source of user frustration into a competitive advantage — turning failures into guided recovery flows.
*   **Action:** I analyzed three months of error logs and categorized every booking failure into one of four buckets: recoverable by the user (62% — things like fare changes, seat unavailability), recoverable by the system automatically (18% — transient API timeouts, retryable payment errors), non-recoverable but explainable (15% — route no longer served, embargo restrictions), and truly unexpected (5%). I authored a technical vision document proposing a "Resilient Booking" architecture with three components: an error classification middleware that categorized each failure in real-time, a recovery flow engine that presented contextual next steps to users (e.g., "this fare expired, here are 3 similar options"), and an automatic retry layer for transient failures with exponential backoff. I presented the vision to the team and product leadership, secured buy-in, and broke it into a three-phase roadmap that we could deliver incrementally — one bucket at a time.
*   **Result:** Over four months, we implemented recovery flows for all four error buckets. Booking completion rate improved by 23%, support calls related to booking errors dropped by 65%, and our NPS score for the booking experience increased by 18 points. The "similar options" recovery flow for expired fares alone recovered an estimated $2.1 million in bookings per quarter that would have previously been abandoned. The product team started calling it the "save the sale" feature and it became a key differentiator in airline partnership pitches.

**Ava:** Tell me about a time you had to hold someone accountable.

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** A senior engineer on my team — technically the strongest coder we had — was consistently dismissive during code reviews. They'd leave terse comments like "wrong" or "why?" without explanation, approve PRs from senior teammates with barely a glance, and block junior engineers' PRs for days with requests for rewrites that reflected stylistic preferences rather than substantive issues. Two junior engineers had separately told me they dreaded getting this person as a reviewer.
*   **Task:** I needed to address the behavior directly while retaining this engineer's valuable technical contributions. They were not being intentionally unkind — they were applying the standards they'd learned at a previous company where terse reviews were the norm — but the impact on the team was real.
*   **Action:** I scheduled a private one-on-one and started by asking about their experience with code reviews — what they valued and what frustrated them. This helped me understand their perspective: they felt reviews were too slow and wanted to be efficient. I then shared the impact their style was having, with specific examples. I showed them three review comments they'd left alongside the junior engineers' reactions (shared anonymously and with the juniors' permission). I made the distinction between high standards — which I valued — and the way those standards were communicated. We agreed on concrete changes: they would write at least one sentence of explanation with every blocking comment, they would focus review feedback on correctness and maintainability rather than style, and they would aim to respond to all PRs within 4 hours rather than letting junior PRs sit while fast-tracking senior ones. I also asked them to mentor one of the junior engineers specifically on the areas where they kept finding issues.
*   **Result:** The change was significant but not overnight. After two weeks, the engineer's review comments became genuinely educational — they started explaining the "why" behind their feedback, and junior engineers began seeking them out as a reviewer rather than avoiding them. The mentorship pairing worked well; the junior engineer's code quality improved noticeably, and the senior engineer told me they found teaching more satisfying than just pointing out problems. Six months later, this engineer's reviews were consistently rated as the most helpful on the team in our anonymous feedback survey.

**Ava:** Share an example of influencing senior stakeholders with limited authority.

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** Our booking engine was running on a single AWS region (eu-west-1). I'd been advocating for multi-region deployment for over a year, but leadership kept deprioritizing it because we'd never had a region-level outage. Meanwhile, our business was expanding into Asia-Pacific, and users in Australia and Singapore were experiencing 400-600ms latency on search queries because every request crossed the globe to Europe. The latency was measurable in our conversion data — APAC booking conversion was 22% lower than European conversion.
*   **Task:** I wanted to reframe the multi-region conversation from disaster recovery (which felt abstract) to revenue growth (which leadership cared about immediately).
*   **Action:** I stopped pitching multi-region as an insurance policy and reframed it as a market expansion enabler. I ran a controlled experiment where I deployed a read-only cache of our flight search index to ap-southeast-1 (Singapore) and routed 10% of APAC search traffic through it for two weeks. Search latency for those users dropped from 500ms to 80ms. I then correlated this with booking conversion data and extrapolated the revenue impact: a 500ms latency reduction for all APAC users would translate to approximately $1.8 million in additional annual bookings based on our conversion-latency model. I presented these findings to the VP of Product and VP of Engineering in a single slide: investment cost (two engineers for six weeks), projected revenue uplift ($1.8M/year), and bonus benefit (DR capability). I also showed a competitive analysis demonstrating that two of our three main competitors already had APAC presence.
*   **Result:** Leadership approved the multi-region project within a week. We deployed our search and booking services to ap-southeast-1 in seven weeks. APAC booking conversion increased by 19%, closely matching my projection. Six months later, when eu-west-1 experienced a 20-minute networking degradation, our APAC users were completely unaffected — the DR benefit we'd gotten "for free." The VP of Product later used the latency-conversion model I'd built as a standard tool for evaluating infrastructure investments.

**Ava:** Describe a time you had to scale operations as your product grew.

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** Our booking engine had grown from handling 30,000 bookings per day to 120,000 in 18 months. Our on-call process hadn't scaled with it. We had a single on-call rotation covering the entire booking stack — from search to payment to post-booking services. The on-call engineer was responsible for 23 different services, many of which they hadn't worked on directly. Alert fatigue was severe — we averaged 40 alerts per on-call shift, but only about 8 required action. The rest were noisy thresholds that hadn't been tuned in over a year. Our last two on-call engineers had requested to be removed from the rotation entirely.
*   **Task:** I needed to redesign our on-call system to be sustainable at our current scale and set up for continued growth.
*   **Action:** I tackled the problem in three layers. First, I spent a week auditing every alert — I categorized each one as "actionable," "informational," or "noise" based on the last three months of on-call logs. I deleted or downgraded 24 of the 40 alerts, tuned thresholds on 8 others based on actual traffic patterns, and consolidated related alerts into grouped notifications. Second, I split the single rotation into two domain-specific rotations — "search and pricing" and "booking and payments" — each covering a smaller, more manageable set of services. Engineers only went on-call for systems they had context on. Third, I created detailed runbooks for every remaining alert, including diagnosis steps, remediation actions, and escalation criteria. Each runbook was co-authored by the engineer who knew the system best and reviewed by someone who didn't, ensuring they were actually followable.
*   **Result:** Alerts per on-call shift dropped from 40 to 9, with 100% of them being actionable. Average incident response time improved from 25 minutes to 8 minutes because engineers were responding to systems they understood using clear runbooks. Both engineers who had requested removal from the rotation voluntarily rejoined. On-call satisfaction scores went from 1.8 to 4.3 out of 5. When we grew to 180,000 bookings per day six months later, we didn't need to revisit the on-call structure — it scaled cleanly.

**Ava:** Tell me about a time you delivered difficult feedback upward to your manager.

**Nico:**
*   **Situation:** My engineering manager had started attending our team's daily standups and jumping in with technical suggestions and task assignments during the meeting. They meant well — they were a former engineer and genuinely wanted to help. But it was undermining my role as Tech Lead. Engineers started directing technical questions to the manager instead of me, and I lost visibility into what people were working on because tasks were being reassigned in standup without my input. The team was confused about who was making technical decisions.
*   **Task:** I needed to have an honest conversation with my manager about role boundaries without damaging our relationship or making them feel like I was pushing them away from the team.
*   **Action:** I requested a one-on-one and started by affirming what I valued about their involvement — their technical instincts were often right, and the team respected their experience. I then described the specific pattern I was seeing: I shared two examples where engineers had received conflicting direction from the two of us, and one where a task reassignment in standup had disrupted a carefully planned sprint dependency. I framed the issue as a role clarity problem, not a personality issue: "The team needs one clear technical decision-maker in the room, and I think we both want that to be the Tech Lead." I proposed that they attend standups as a listener and raise any technical concerns with me afterward, so I could incorporate their input and present a unified direction to the team. For topics where they wanted to be more hands-on, I suggested they join our design reviews, where their architectural experience was genuinely valuable.
*   **Result:** My manager appreciated the directness and agreed to the new approach. They later told me they hadn't realized the impact because no one else had said anything. The role clarity improved immediately — engineers stopped going around me, and I regained full visibility into the team's work. My manager became one of our most valuable design review participants, contributing insights that improved several critical designs. Our working relationship actually strengthened because we'd demonstrated we could have hard conversations productively.
