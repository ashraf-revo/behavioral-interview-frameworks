# Mock Interview: Tech Lead

**Candidate:** Yuki, a software engineer with 7 years of experience, with the last 2 as a Tech Lead at a logistics technology company.

**Interviewer:** Sandra, a Director of Engineering at a mid-size B2B SaaS company.

---

### Introduction

**Sandra:** Hi Yuki, thanks for coming in today. I'm Sandra, Director of Engineering here. I lead our platform and integrations group — about 30 engineers across four teams. Could you give me an overview of your background?

**Yuki:** Hi Sandra, great to meet you. I've been in software for about seven years. I started as a frontend engineer building React applications at a digital agency, then transitioned to full-stack work at a logistics tech company where I've been for the past four years. Two years ago I stepped into the Tech Lead role for our shipment tracking team — five engineers building real-time tracking and notification systems that handle about 2 million shipment events per day. I'm responsible for the team's technical direction, delivery execution, and growing the engineers on the team. I'm looking for a role where I can lead a larger team and tackle more complex cross-team technical challenges.

---

### Behavioral Questions

**Sandra:** Great. Let's start with leadership. Describe your leadership style with a specific example.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** When I took over as Tech Lead, the team had a pattern of one engineer — the most senior person — making all architectural decisions. The other four engineers would implement what was prescribed without deeply understanding the "why." When that senior engineer went on leave for a month, the team was paralyzed on any decision that required design judgment.
*   **Task:** I wanted to build a team where every engineer could reason about technical trade-offs, not just execute predefined solutions. My leadership style centers on building capability, not dependency.
*   **Action:** I introduced "design ownership rotation" — for each new feature, a different engineer was responsible for writing the technical approach document, regardless of seniority. I'd review their document with them one-on-one before the team discussion, asking questions like "what alternatives did you consider?" and "what happens if this assumption is wrong?" rather than giving answers. During team design reviews, I facilitated discussion but held back my own opinion until everyone else had weighed in. For the first two rotations, I paired closely with the junior engineers to build their confidence. I also started sharing my own decision-making process transparently — when I made a technical call, I'd explain the trade-offs I weighed and what would make me reverse the decision.
*   **Result:** Within three months, every engineer on the team had authored at least two design documents. When the senior engineer returned from leave, they commented that the team felt noticeably more self-directed. Our design review discussions became richer — junior engineers started catching edge cases that senior members had overlooked. When I was out for a week, the team designed and shipped a notification batching feature without needing my input at all.

**Sandra:** Tell me about a time you had to make a trade-off between people, time, and scope.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** We were building a real-time shipment ETA prediction feature for our enterprise customers. The project had a firm deadline because it was written into a contract renewal worth $3.2 million annually with our largest client. Four months into the six-month project, one of my five engineers accepted an offer at another company and gave two weeks' notice. Around the same time, another engineer disclosed that they were experiencing burnout and asked to reduce their hours temporarily.
*   **Task:** I needed to deliver the core ETA prediction feature by the contract deadline with effectively three engineers at full capacity, down from five.
*   **Action:** I sat down with the product manager and mapped every remaining work item to one of three categories: "must have for contract" (accurate ETA predictions via API), "important but deferrable" (a customer-facing dashboard for ETA visualization), and "nice to have" (historical accuracy analytics). I was transparent with the team about the situation and asked for their input on what was feasible. Together, we decided to defer the dashboard and analytics — the API-only delivery still met the contract requirements. I also took on the departing engineer's in-progress work myself rather than context-switching a remaining engineer. For the team member experiencing burnout, I restructured their tasks to focus on well-scoped, independent work with clear boundaries so they could maintain sustainable hours.
*   **Result:** We delivered the ETA prediction API one week before the contract deadline. The client renewed the contract. The deferred dashboard shipped the following quarter. The engineer who was experiencing burnout told me during their next one-on-one that the restructured workload had been exactly what they needed — they recovered and returned to full capacity within six weeks. We backfilled the departed engineer's position within two months.

**Sandra:** How do you handle technical disagreements within your team?

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** Two engineers on my team had a persistent disagreement about our event processing architecture. One wanted to adopt Apache Kafka to replace our existing RabbitMQ setup, arguing that Kafka's log-based model was better suited for our event replay and audit requirements. The other argued that RabbitMQ was working fine, the team already knew how to operate it, and switching would introduce risk with no immediate business value. The disagreement had surfaced in three consecutive design reviews and was starting to create tension.
*   **Task:** I needed to help the team reach a resolution that was technically sound, backed by evidence rather than preferences, and that both engineers could commit to.
*   **Action:** I acknowledged that both engineers had valid points and proposed a structured evaluation. I asked each of them to own one side of a comparison document addressing five criteria: our actual event replay requirements (how far back, how often), operational complexity with our current team's expertise, performance under our load patterns, cost, and migration risk. I gave them one week and asked them to include concrete data, not projections. I also brought in our SRE team to provide an independent assessment of operational readiness for each option. We held a dedicated 90-minute review session where I facilitated the discussion, keeping it focused on the data.
*   **Result:** The evaluation revealed that our event replay requirement — replaying up to 7 days of shipment events for debugging — was poorly served by RabbitMQ's queue semantics, which was the root cause of a recurring operational pain point the team had been working around manually. However, the data also showed that a full Kafka migration was overkill for our current scale. We agreed on a middle path: adopting Kafka for the event replay use case only, while keeping RabbitMQ for our standard async messaging where it worked well. Both engineers felt their concerns were heard, and the hybrid approach gave us the benefits without the full migration risk.

**Sandra:** Tell me about a time you improved an agile process that wasn't working.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** Our team's code reviews were a significant bottleneck. Engineers would open a pull request and then wait an average of 18 hours for the first review. Some PRs sat for two or three days. This was killing our cycle time — work that should take a day to merge was taking three to four days. Engineers started batching their work into larger PRs to avoid the overhead of multiple review cycles, which made the problem worse because large PRs took even longer to review.
*   **Task:** I needed to fix our review process to unblock delivery without mandating a specific workflow that the team would resent.
*   **Action:** I brought the data to the team — average review wait time, PR size distribution, and cycle time trends — during a retro and asked the team to help solve it. Together, we decided on three changes. First, we adopted a "review hour" — the first hour of every morning was reserved for reviewing open PRs before starting new work. Second, we set a team guideline of 400 lines maximum per PR (excluding generated code), with the understanding that smaller PRs get reviewed faster. Third, I configured our GitHub bot to auto-assign two reviewers based on code ownership, removing the ambiguity of "someone will get to it." I also led by example — I committed to reviewing any PR assigned to me within four hours.
*   **Result:** Within one month, our average first-review time dropped from 18 hours to 3 hours. Average PR size decreased from 650 lines to 280 lines. Our overall cycle time — from first commit to production — went from 4.2 days to 1.8 days. The team cited the review hour as one of their favorite process changes in the quarterly survey, and two other teams in the company adopted it after hearing about our results.

**Sandra:** Describe how you manage sprint ceremonies to ensure they are productive.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** When I became Tech Lead, our sprint planning meetings were 90-minute marathons. Engineers would debate implementation details during estimation, backlog items were frequently underspecified, and we'd leave planning without a clear commitment — just a vague list of items to "try to get to."
*   **Task:** I needed to restructure our sprint ceremonies to be focused and time-efficient while actually producing clear commitments.
*   **Action:** I separated the work into three distinct activities. First, I introduced a weekly 30-minute "backlog refinement" session mid-sprint where we'd review upcoming items, clarify requirements with the product manager, and flag technical unknowns. This meant items were well-understood before they ever entered sprint planning. Second, I restructured sprint planning itself to a strict 45-minute format: 10 minutes reviewing the sprint goal, 25 minutes selecting and committing to stories (estimation happened during refinement), and 10 minutes identifying risks and dependencies. Third, I added a "sprint contract" ritual — at the end of planning, I'd read back the committed stories and the team would explicitly agree or raise concerns. I also started ending each standup with a quick check: "are we on track for the sprint contract?"
*   **Result:** Sprint planning went from 90 minutes to 45 minutes. Our sprint completion rate — the percentage of committed stories actually delivered — went from 55% to 88%. Engineers stopped dreading planning meetings and the product manager said it was the most reliable team they worked with. The "sprint contract" concept gave us a shared language for discussing scope changes mid-sprint — instead of silently absorbing new requests, the team would say "that's a sprint contract change" and we'd consciously discuss the trade-off.

**Sandra:** Give an example of when you set the direction for a team or project.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** Our shipment tracking system was built as a polling architecture — our services would poll carrier APIs every 15 minutes for status updates. As we grew from 50,000 to 2 million daily shipments, the polling approach was hitting carrier rate limits, creating 15-minute data staleness, and consuming disproportionate compute resources. Multiple engineers had proposed point fixes — smarter polling intervals, caching, carrier-specific optimizations — but no one had stepped back to define a comprehensive technical direction.
*   **Task:** I needed to set a technical vision for the tracking system that would scale with our growth trajectory and shift us from a reactive, patch-by-patch approach to a deliberate architecture.
*   **Action:** I wrote a technical vision document outlining a shift from polling to an event-driven architecture. The core idea was to adopt carrier webhook integrations where available (covering about 70% of our volume) and use intelligent adaptive polling only for carriers that didn't support webhooks. The adaptive polling would adjust frequency based on a shipment's lifecycle stage — polling every hour during transit but every minute when a delivery was imminent. I included capacity projections, a cost-benefit analysis showing the compute savings, and a phased migration plan that let us convert one carrier at a time without disrupting the existing system. I presented the vision to the team, our product manager, and the VP of Engineering, incorporating feedback from each audience into the final plan.
*   **Result:** The team rallied around the vision because it addressed pain points they'd been living with daily. Over five months, we migrated our top six carriers (representing 80% of volume) to webhook-based tracking. Polling API calls dropped by 75%, compute costs for the tracking service decreased by 60%, and shipment status latency went from 15 minutes to under 30 seconds for webhook-integrated carriers. The VP of Engineering referenced the vision document as an example of how Tech Leads should approach architectural direction.

**Sandra:** Tell me about a time you had to hold someone accountable.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** A mid-level engineer on my team was consistently missing their self-set estimates — not by small margins, but by 50-100%. They would commit to delivering a feature by Thursday and it would arrive the following Wednesday. The pattern had continued for three sprints. Other team members were starting to plan around the unreliability, which was creating resentment.
*   **Task:** I needed to address the pattern directly, understand the root cause, and help the engineer improve — while being fair and supportive rather than punitive.
*   **Action:** I scheduled a private one-on-one and opened by sharing the data: in the last three sprints, five out of seven of their committed items had been delivered significantly late. I asked open-ended questions to understand what was happening. It turned out the engineer was struggling with two things: they felt pressure to give optimistic estimates during planning because they didn't want to seem slow, and they were getting stuck on implementation details but not asking for help until they'd already lost days. Together, we agreed on three concrete actions. First, I'd privately review their estimates with them before sprint planning so they could adjust without peer pressure. Second, they would post a daily update in our team channel with a confidence level (green/yellow/red) on their current task, and if it turned yellow, I'd check in to see if pairing would help. Third, we set a "24-hour stuck rule" — if they were stuck on something for more than a day, they'd reach out to me or a teammate.
*   **Result:** Over the next two sprints, their delivery predictability improved significantly — they missed only one estimate, and by a single day. The daily confidence signals caught two potential delays early, and in both cases a 30-minute pairing session unblocked them. Their teammates noticed the improvement and the resentment dissolved. The engineer later told me that the private estimate review was the key — it removed the anxiety that was causing them to commit to unrealistic timelines.

**Sandra:** Share an example of influencing senior stakeholders with limited authority.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** Our logistics platform had no automated disaster recovery testing. We ran on AWS, but our RDS databases, ElastiCache clusters, and S3 buckets had never been tested for cross-region failover. I'd raised this in quarterly planning twice, but leadership viewed it as an insurance policy they didn't need yet — we'd never had a major outage, so it felt like a theoretical risk. Then AWS us-east-1 had a significant service degradation that affected our competitors (we happened to be in us-west-2 and were fine), and suddenly leadership was asking "are we prepared for this?"
*   **Task:** I wanted to use the moment to secure investment in DR capabilities without resorting to fear-mongering — I wanted leadership to understand the actual risk and make an informed decision.
*   **Action:** I prepared a concise one-page risk assessment with three sections. First, I listed our critical dependencies and their current failover capabilities (most had none). Second, I estimated the business cost of a 4-hour outage based on our average hourly revenue, SLA penalty exposure, and customer churn risk — the total came to approximately $480,000. Third, I proposed a phased plan: a 2-week spike to set up cross-region RDS replicas and test failover for our most critical service, followed by a quarterly DR drill program. I timed the presentation for our next leadership sync while the AWS incident was still fresh, and I framed the investment as $40,000 in engineering time to protect against a $480,000+ exposure.
*   **Result:** Leadership approved the phase-one DR work immediately. We successfully tested cross-region failover for our core tracking service within three weeks. Six months later, when us-west-2 experienced a 45-minute EBS degradation, we failed over to us-east-1 in under 10 minutes with no customer impact. The VP of Engineering sent a company-wide message crediting the DR investment. The quarterly DR drills became a standard practice.

**Sandra:** Describe a time you had to scale processes, hiring, or operations.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** My team was growing from five to eight engineers as the company expanded our tracking product line. I was heavily involved in every hire — screening resumes, conducting all technical interviews, and making final decisions. For our first two hires, this worked fine, but now I had three open roles to fill simultaneously while still leading the team's daily delivery work.
*   **Task:** I needed to scale our hiring process so that it didn't depend solely on me, while maintaining the quality bar the team expected.
*   **Action:** I created a structured interview kit for the team. I designed a take-home assignment based on a simplified version of a real problem we'd solved — building a shipment event processor with specific edge cases around out-of-order events and duplicate detection. I wrote a detailed rubric with examples of what "meets bar," "exceeds bar," and "does not meet" looked like for each evaluation criterion. I then trained two senior engineers on the team to conduct the system design interview, doing a mock interview with each of them and providing feedback on their evaluation technique. I kept the final-round cultural and team-fit conversation as my responsibility but delegated the technical evaluation entirely.
*   **Result:** We filled all three positions in eight weeks. The two engineers I trained conducted 12 technical interviews between them and their evaluations were well-calibrated — there were no surprises in the final round. The take-home assignment doubled as a realistic job preview, and all three new hires mentioned it positively as a factor in choosing our company. The interview kit and rubric continued to be used after I moved on, meaning the hiring process was no longer dependent on any single person.

**Sandra:** Finally, tell me about a time you had to deliver difficult feedback to someone more senior than you.

**Yuki:**
*   **Situation:** Our product manager — who had significantly more tenure and organizational influence than I did — had a habit of going directly to individual engineers on my team with urgent requests, bypassing me and the sprint process. Engineers would context-switch to address the PM's requests, disrupting their current work, and I'd find out days later when a committed sprint item was delayed. The PM wasn't being malicious — they were just used to a more ad-hoc working style from before the team had a Tech Lead.
*   **Task:** I needed to redirect the PM's behavior without creating an adversarial relationship — we had to work together closely every day and I respected their product expertise.
*   **Action:** I asked the PM for a 1:1 over coffee, framing it as "I want to make sure we're working together as effectively as possible." I started by acknowledging the value of their urgency — their instinct to unblock things quickly was something I admired. Then I shared the impact of the direct requests: I showed three specific examples from the past month where a direct ask had caused a sprint item to slip, and explained that the engineers felt caught between competing priorities. I proposed a simple protocol — for anything urgent, the PM would message me first, and I'd either re-prioritize immediately or explain the trade-off so we could decide together. For planned work, everything would go through backlog refinement as usual.
*   **Result:** The PM was receptive — they genuinely hadn't realized the impact of their approach. We adopted the protocol, and it worked well. Urgent requests still got handled quickly (usually within hours), but with visibility and conscious trade-off decisions. The PM later told me it actually made their life easier too, because I'd often suggest simpler solutions or point out when a "urgent" request was already covered by existing work. Our working relationship became one of the strongest PM-Tech Lead partnerships in the company.
