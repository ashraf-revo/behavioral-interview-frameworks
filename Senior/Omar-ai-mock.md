### Behavioral Questions

**Lin:** Great background. Let's start — tell me about a recent project you owned end-to-end. What was the outcome?

**Omar:**
*   **Situation:** Our scheduling engine had a significant scalability problem. When a clinic with 50+ providers opened their schedule for a new month, the availability calculation would take up to 12 seconds per provider — multiplied across 50 providers, the page would time out. This happened every month for our largest clients, and the workaround was our support team manually staggering when each clinic's schedule was published. We were losing enterprise deals because prospects would see the timeout during demos.
*   **Task:** I owned redesigning the availability calculation to handle bulk schedule openings without timeouts while maintaining correctness — in healthcare, showing a wrong available slot can result in double-bookings that affect patient care.
*   **Action:** I profiled the existing calculation and found two bottlenecks: it was recalculating every provider's availability from scratch by joining six tables for every request, and it held a database-level advisory lock during the calculation to prevent concurrent booking conflicts. I redesigned the system around an event-sourced availability model — instead of computing availability on-demand, we maintained a materialized view that was updated incrementally whenever an appointment was booked, cancelled, or a schedule was modified. For the concurrency problem, I replaced the coarse database lock with optimistic concurrency control using version vectors on each time slot. I built the new system alongside the old one, ran both in parallel for two weeks comparing outputs to validate correctness, and then cut over with a feature flag.
*   **Result:** Availability calculations for a 50-provider clinic went from 10 minutes of wall-clock time to under 2 seconds. The advisory lock removal also improved our p99 booking latency from 1.8 seconds to 200ms because bookings no longer waited on availability calculations. We signed three new enterprise clients in the following quarter who had previously rejected us due to the timeout issue. The parallel validation approach caught two edge cases in my new implementation before they reached production.

**Lin:** Describe a tough decision you made with incomplete information.

**Omar:**
*   **Situation:** Our appointment reminder system sent SMS and email notifications to patients. We received reports from three clinics that some patients weren't receiving their SMS reminders. Our SMS provider's dashboard showed all messages as "delivered," so from their perspective nothing was wrong. I had no way to confirm whether the messages were actually reaching patients' phones — the "delivered" status only meant the carrier accepted the message, not that the patient's device received it. The issue was intermittent and affected roughly 5% of messages.
*   **Task:** I needed to decide on a course of action knowing I couldn't definitively diagnose the root cause — we couldn't access carrier-level delivery data, and the intermittent nature made it hard to reproduce.
*   **Action:** I considered three hypotheses: our SMS provider was having delivery issues, the affected phone numbers had carrier-level filtering (common with short codes), or our message queue was silently dropping messages under load. I couldn't prove any of these definitively, so I chose the approach that would address the most likely cause while improving our system regardless of the root cause. I implemented a dual-delivery strategy: we would send critical appointment reminders via both SMS and email, and I added delivery receipt tracking by implementing a webhook listener for actual device delivery confirmations (not just carrier acceptance). I also added a "reminder confirmation" feature where patients could tap a link to confirm they received the reminder, giving us end-to-end visibility for the first time.
*   **Result:** The dual-delivery approach immediately eliminated the patient impact — even when SMS failed, email got through. The delivery receipt webhook revealed that the actual failure rate was 8%, not 5%, and was concentrated with one specific carrier. This data let us file a targeted support ticket with our SMS provider, who discovered that our short code had been flagged by that carrier's spam filter. They resolved it within a week. The reminder confirmation feature became a popular product feature in its own right — clinics loved seeing confirmation rates, and it reduced no-show rates by 11% because patients felt more engaged.

**Lin:** Tell me about a time you persuaded someone to change their mind.

**Omar:**
*   **Situation:** Our product manager wanted to implement a waitlist feature where patients could join a waitlist for a fully-booked provider and get notified when a slot opened up. Their proposed design was to scan all waitlists every 5 minutes and match them against current availability. I was concerned because this approach would create a thundering herd problem — when a popular provider had a cancellation, hundreds of patients would receive simultaneous notifications, and the first one to click would book the slot while everyone else would see an "unavailable" error. This would be a terrible user experience and would generate a spike of database writes as hundreds of patients tried to book simultaneously.
*   **Task:** I needed to convince the PM that the simple polling approach, while easy to understand, would create a bad user experience and propose an alternative.
*   **Action:** Instead of just describing the problem theoretically, I built a quick simulation. I wrote a script that modeled 200 patients on a waitlist, simulated a cancellation, and showed what would happen: 200 notifications sent, 200 near-simultaneous booking attempts, 1 success, 199 error screens, and a 30-second database write spike. I then presented an alternative design: a priority queue-based system where patients were ranked by criteria the clinic could configure (e.g., longest waiting, closest to the clinic, most urgent need), and when a slot opened, we'd offer it to the top patient for 10 minutes before moving to the next. I showed the PM both flows side-by-side — the "free-for-all" versus the "orderly queue" — and let them see which one they'd prefer as a patient.
*   **Result:** The PM immediately understood the problem once they saw the simulation. They not only agreed to the priority queue approach but got excited about it as a differentiating feature — clinics could use the priority criteria to align waitlist behavior with their care philosophy. The waitlist feature launched with the priority queue design and became one of our most-requested features. Clinics that enabled it saw a 28% reduction in unfilled cancellation slots because the orderly notification flow converted at a much higher rate than a race-to-book model would have.

The feature launched with sub-100ms search times and has been reliable for two years.
